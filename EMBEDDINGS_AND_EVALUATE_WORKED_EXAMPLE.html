WHERE MODULE 4 LEFT OFF

TF–IDF only sees the exact words

TF–IDF scored a course by how many of Riya's exact gap words it contained. That means a course can be about precisely the right topic and still score zero — simply because it phrased things differently.

The blind spot in one line: to TF–IDF, "ML", "neural networks" and "machine learning" are three unrelated strings of letters. A great course that says "neural networks" instead of "machine learning" gets 0 — and never reaches Riya. Module 5 is built to fix exactly this.

The one thing that changes: only Step 1 is new. Steps 2 and 3 (cosine similarity → sort → top-K) are identical to Module 4. So the two recommenders differ in exactly one place: how text becomes numbers — by counting words (TF–IDF) or by reading meaning (embeddings).

What is an "embedding"?

Think of a huge map of topics. A pretrained model (we use a small, free one called all-MiniLM-L6-v2) places every course as a dot on this map, so that courses about similar things sit close together — even if they never share a single word. An embedding is just that dot's position, written as a list of numbers. "Close on the map" = "similar meaning".

MODULE 6 · THE CORRECT ANSWER

To grade a list, first define "relevant"

You cannot say which recommender is better until you agree what a good recommendation is. We fix one simple, honest rule — the same one for both methods — called is_relevant().

The rule: a course counts as relevant if any of Riya's five gap skills appears — as a whole word — in that course's skills. Nothing fancier.

Why "whole word"? So statistics correctly matches the course phrase "statistics probability data analysis". Whole-word matching keeps it honest — it counts a real skill match, not an accidental letter overlap.

MODULE 6 · THE SCORE

precision@k — the fraction that were right

precision@k asks a single question: of the top k courses a method recommended, how many were actually relevant? Count the ✓, divide by k. That's it.

precision@5 for the embedding list — its top 5 are Data Visualization ✓, Deep Learning ✓, SQL ✓, Statistics ✓, Machine Learning ✓ → 5 relevant out of 5 → precision@5 = 5 ÷ 5 = 1.00. TF–IDF's top 5 are the same five courses in a different order → also 1.00.

What a "miss" would look like: stretch to the top 6 and Yoga gets pulled in — one ✕. Now it's 5 right out of 6 → precision@6 = 0.83. That is how an off-topic recommendation drags the score down. (A perfect 1.00 just means no junk in the top k.)

MODULE 6 · THE HONEST CATCH

Sometimes the number can't pick a winner

On our toy six, both methods scored precision@5 = 1.00 — a tie. And here is the honest part you should put in your report: on the full 623-course dataset, both methods also score precision@10 = 1.00 for Riya. A tie again.

Why the tie? Our is_relevant() rule is generous — with 600+ courses, almost anything either method puts in the top 10 happens to contain one of the gap skills, so both look "perfect". precision@k simply isn't sharp enough to separate them here. This is a real limitation, not a mistake — name it.
So what's actually different? Not the score — the courses. TF–IDF surfaces literal keyword matches; embeddings surface topically-related courses (some with no shared words at all). The right conclusion isn't "X wins by the number" — it's "here's how the two lists differ, and here's when you'd trust each."
